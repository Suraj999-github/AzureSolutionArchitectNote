Section 01

Overview & Industry Context

International remittance refers to the transfer of funds from one country to another, typically by migrant workers sending money to their home countries. According to the World Bank, remittance flows to low- and middle-income countries exceed $650 billion annually, making this one of the most significant financial flows globally.

A remittance system is a multi-party, multi-jurisdiction financial infrastructure that facilitates the movement of value across borders. It involves the sending entity (sender), receiving entity (recipient), originating payment service provider (PSP), correspondent or partner network, and the beneficiary PSP. Each transaction must navigate regulatory requirements in at minimum two jurisdictions.

Key Participants in a Remittance Network

Participant Role Regulatory Obligation
Sender (Remitter) Initiates the transfer from the source country Subject to KYC, AML, transaction limits in source country
Originating PSP / MSB Licensed money service business accepting funds from sender Must hold MTL/MSB license; file SARs, CTRs; comply with BSA/FinCEN (US) or equivalent
Corridor Partner / Agent Pays out funds in the destination country Must be licensed in destination country; performs local KYC on beneficiary
Beneficiary / Recipient Receives funds in destination country May be subject to local KYC for cash payouts above thresholds
Correspondent Bank Holds Nostro/Vostro accounts; facilitates cross-border settlement Subject to home-country banking regulations; screens for sanctions
Regulatory Bodies FINCEN, FCA, MAS, RBI, NBP, etc. — oversee licensing and compliance Define AML/CFT rules, reporting thresholds, licensing requirements

Remittance Business Models

Modern remittance operators generally fall into one of three models:

  • Bank-led model: The sending bank directly initiates a SWIFT MT103 or SWIFT gpi message through its correspondent banking network. Used for wire transfers.
  • Non-bank PSP / MTL holder model: A licensed money transmitter (e.g., Western Union, Remitly) collects funds locally, pools them, and pays out through local agents or bank partners. No individual SWIFT per transaction in real-time.
  • Fintech / API-driven model: Modern operators (e.g., Wise, Remitly) use local collection accounts in each corridor and net settle, using the mid-market FX rate and domestic transfers on both ends to minimize cost.
Section 02

Outbound Remittance Flow

The outbound flow represents the full journey from customer onboarding through to fund dispatch from the sending country. This section documents each stage in operational detail.

Stage 1: Customer Onboarding & Identity Verification

  1. 1
    Customer Registration Customer provides full legal name, date of birth, residential address, nationality, contact details, and government-issued ID. A unique customer profile (CIF — Customer Information File) is created in the core system.
  2. 2
    KYC Verification (Know Your Customer) Document verification via OCR + liveness check (eKYC). Identity documents cross-referenced against government databases, PEP (Politically Exposed Persons) lists, and adverse media databases. Risk scoring assigns the customer a risk tier: Low / Medium / High. Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) required for High-risk customers.
  3. 3
    OFAC / Sanctions Screening (Onboarding) Customer name and identifiers screened against OFAC SDN list (US), HM Treasury list (UK), EU Consolidated Sanctions List, UN Consolidated List. Fuzzy matching algorithms used (Jaro-Winkler, Levenshtein distance) to catch name variations. Positive hits trigger a manual review queue. False positives resolved and documented.
  4. 4
    Source of Funds & Wealth Declaration For high-risk profiles or high-value senders, source of funds documentation required (payslips, tax returns, business documents). This satisfies FATF Recommendation 10 for CDD.
  5. 5
    Bank / Payment Method Verification Linked bank account verified via micro-deposit, open banking (Plaid/TrueLayer), or instant bank verification. Card details tokenized via PCI-DSS compliant vault (never stored in plain text). Debit/credit card BIN validation, 3DS2 authentication for card payments.
  6. 6
    Transaction Limit Assignment Based on KYC tier, the customer is assigned daily, weekly, and per-transaction send limits. Unverified accounts may be restricted to lower thresholds (e.g., $999/day to stay below CTR filing triggers). Fully verified accounts receive higher limits. Velocity rules tracked in real-time.

Stage 2: Transaction Initiation

  1. 7
    Recipient / Beneficiary Setup Sender adds recipient: full name, address, bank account number (IBAN or local format), bank BIC/SWIFT code, and relationship to sender. Recipient details validated against local bank routing databases (SWIFT BIC directory, national bank registries). Account number format validated per destination country rules (e.g., IBAN checksum for SEPA countries).
  2. 8
    FX Rate Quote Generation System fetches live mid-market rates from providers (Reuters Eikon, Bloomberg FX, OpenExchangeRates, internal treasury pricing). Applies the operator's spread or service fee. Rate is locked for a defined validity window (typically 30 seconds to 5 minutes). Rate snapshot stored with rate source, timestamp, mid-rate, and applied rate for audit trail. FX rate risk is managed by the operator's treasury function.
  3. 9
    Fee Calculation & Disclosure Total cost disclosed upfront (per regulatory requirements including CFPB Remittance Transfer Rule in the US, PSD2 in EU): send amount, fees, FX rate, recipient receives amount, estimated delivery time, and exchange rate expiry. This pre-transaction disclosure is a legal requirement in most regulated markets.
  4. 10
    Real-Time Fraud Scoring ML-based fraud model evaluates: device fingerprint, IP geolocation vs. registered address, behavioral biometrics (typing speed, session behavior), transaction velocity (frequency, amounts), recipient risk profile, corridor risk (high-risk corridors flagged), time-of-day patterns, and historical user behavior. Score determines: auto-approve, 3DS challenge, step-up verification, or decline.

Stage 3: Pre-Transaction Validation

  1. 11
    Compliance Rule Engine Transaction evaluated against configurable rule sets: corridor-specific limits and restrictions (some corridors may be blocked), product rules (cash payout vs. bank deposit vs. mobile wallet), regulatory limits (CTR triggers at $10,000 in the US), sending frequency rules, recipient country sanctions status, and operator-defined business rules.
  2. 12
    Transaction-Level OFAC / Sanctions Screening Both sender and recipient screened at transaction time (not just at onboarding). Recipient name, bank details, and country cross-checked against all applicable sanctions lists. Blocked transactions placed in pending-review queue; compliance officer notified. Regulatory hold can last up to 5 business days during review.
  3. 13
    AML Transaction Monitoring (Pre-Submission) Rule-based and ML transaction monitoring: structuring detection (multiple transactions below CTR threshold), unusual pattern detection (spike in frequency or amount), high-risk beneficiary country check, PEP/adverse media check on recipient. Suspicious transactions flagged for SAR consideration; not blocked unless there is a regulatory hold obligation.
  4. 14
    Sender Balance / Funding Verification For wallet-funded transactions: real-time balance check against sender's stored-value account. For bank-funded: payment authorization pre-check (card auth hold or ACH notification of credit). Insufficient funds results in immediate decline with clear error code and user notification.
  5. 15
    Limit & Velocity Check Aggregated transaction counters checked: daily/weekly/monthly sent amount, number of transactions in rolling period, recipient-level limits. Checks are atomic to prevent race conditions in high-concurrency environments. Distributed locks (e.g., Redis) used for real-time counter updates.

Stage 4: Transaction Creation & Dispatch

  1. 16
    Transaction Record Creation Immutable transaction record created in database with unique transaction ID (UUID), status PENDING, locked FX rate, fees, send amount, payout amount, sender/recipient details, corridor reference, and compliance check results. All fields are write-once; state transitions are append-only in an audit log table.
  2. 17
    Funds Collection (Debit) Customer's payment method is charged: card authorization capture, ACH debit submission, or wallet balance debit. Funds moved to the operator's pooled collection account (not yet disbursed). This step is idempotent — retries will not result in double charges (idempotency key pattern).
  3. 18
    Message Queue Submission (Async Processing) Transaction event published to a message broker (e.g., Apache Kafka, RabbitMQ, AWS SQS). Consumers include: partner API dispatcher, accounting service, notification service, AML monitoring service, and analytics pipeline. Decoupling via event-driven architecture ensures resilience — partner API failures do not affect the collection step.
  4. 19
    Partner / Correspondent API Submission Transaction payload sent to payout partner API (may be a bank, fintech payout aggregator, or proprietary agent network). Payload includes: transaction ID, recipient bank details, payout amount in local currency, sender reference, and compliance attestation. Partner acknowledges with their reference ID. Transaction status updated to PROCESSING.
  5. 20
    Accounting / Voucher Entry Double-entry bookkeeping entries created: Debit — Customer Liability / Collection Account; Credit — Nostro Account / Partner Payable. These entries reflect the operator's obligation to deliver funds. Entries posted to the general ledger in real time or in near-real-time batch, depending on system architecture.
  6. 21
    Sender Notification Customer notified via email, SMS, and/or push notification with: confirmation number, expected delivery time, locked exchange rate, recipient name, and payout amount. Regulatory receipt generated (required under CFPB Rule / PSD2) with cancellation rights and contact information for disputes.

Stage 5: Status Tracking & Completion

  1. 22
    Status Polling / Webhook System polls partner for status updates via periodic API calls or receives webhook notifications. Status transitions: PROCESSING → PAID, PROCESSING → FAILED, PROCESSING → ON_HOLD. Retry logic with exponential backoff for failed polls. Dead-letter queue for unresolvable partner communication failures.
  2. 23
    Post-Transaction AML Monitoring Completed transaction fed into batch AML analysis: pattern analysis across customer portfolio, STR/SAR consideration for suspicious patterns, CTR filing if aggregate daily cash exceeds $10,000 (US), FBAR obligations for large cross-border transactions. AML alerts reviewed by compliance team within defined SLA.
  3. 24
    Liquidity Management Update Treasury notified of disbursed amount per corridor. Nostro account balance updated. If corridor balance falls below pre-defined threshold, treasury initiates replenishment transfer to corridor partner. Liquidity positions tracked in real-time dashboard across all corridors.
Section 03

Inbound Remittance Flow

The inbound flow describes the process from the perspective of the receiving country — from the moment the payout instruction arrives at the correspondent/agent to the moment funds reach the recipient.

Stage 1: Instruction Receipt & Validation

  1. 1
    Payout Instruction Receipt Payout partner in the receiving country receives the transaction instruction from the originating PSP via API, SWIFT MT103, ISO 20022 pacs.008, or proprietary message format. Instruction contains: unique reference ID, recipient name, account details, payout amount in local currency, and sender reference.
  2. 2
    Inbound Compliance Screening Receiving-country regulations require a fresh screening at the payout end. Recipient name screened against local sanctions lists and any applicable OFAC/UN lists. Inbound transaction value checked against local threshold reporting requirements (e.g., FINTRAC in Canada, AUSTRAC in Australia, FIU-IND in India). Suspicious patterns flagged to local financial intelligence unit.
  3. 3
    Recipient Account Validation Bank account number validated: IBAN checksum (Europe), IFSC + account number (India), BSB + account number (Australia), sort code + account (UK). Some corridors support pre-validation via national bank APIs (e.g., India's IMPS name-matching, UK's Confirmation of Payee). Invalid account details result in a PAYOUT_FAILED status returned to originating PSP.
  4. 4
    Local FX Conversion (if applicable) If the payout instruction arrives in a foreign currency and local conversion is needed (e.g., USD instruction to PHP payout), the payout partner applies the agreed exchange rate from the corridor contract. Some corridors operate on a pre-funded USD basis; others net-settle in local currency.

Stage 2: Payout Execution

  1. 5
    Liquidity Check at Payout Partner Payout partner checks their local nostro/prefunded account balance to ensure sufficient local currency is available. If balance is low, payout may be queued pending liquidity replenishment. This is a key operational risk managed through daily liquidity reporting and rolling forecasts.
  2. 6
    Payout Rail Selection System selects the optimal local payout rail based on: recipient account type, amount, time-of-day (cut-off times), and rail availability. Options include: real-time gross settlement (RTGS — for high-value), automated clearing house (ACH/NEFT/BACS — for standard), instant payment networks (UPI in India, FPS in UK, RTP in US, PromptPay in Thailand), or cash-agent network for cash payout.
  3. 7
    Credit Instruction Submission Payout partner submits the credit instruction to the local interbank network. Instruction carries originator reference for end-to-end traceability. ISO 20022 pain.001 / pacs.008 used in modern implementations. Acknowledgement received from local bank or clearing system.
  4. 8
    Recipient Account Credit Recipient's bank account credited. For instant rails, this happens in seconds. For batch rails (ACH, NEFT), credit occurs at next settlement cycle (T+0 to T+2 depending on rail and country). Mobile wallet payouts credited directly to the wallet provider's API.
  5. 9
    Recipient Notification Recipient notified via SMS (most common in developing corridors), email, or app notification. Message includes: amount credited, sender name (if disclosed), reference number. In some markets, the originating PSP also triggers a recipient notification via a separate channel.

Stage 3: Confirmation & Reconciliation

  1. 10
    Status Confirmation to Originating PSP Payout partner returns a PAID confirmation with: payout timestamp, local transaction reference, payout amount, and recipient account last digits. This triggers the final status update in the originating PSP system and confirmation notification to the sender.
  2. 11
    Settlement Instruction Payout partner records the disbursed amount against the originating PSP's pre-funded account (Nostro/Vostro balance deduction). At end-of-day, a net settlement file is generated. The originating PSP reconciles the settlement file against its own transaction records and initiates replenishment if below minimum balance threshold.
  3. 12
    Exception Handling — Returned Payments If a payout fails post-submission (e.g., closed account, incorrect details, beneficiary deceased), the payout partner returns funds and issues a return notification. Originating PSP initiates a refund to sender, reversing accounting entries and notifying the customer. Refund processing time varies by funding method: card refunds 3–5 business days, wallet refunds are instant.

Section 05

Technical Architecture

Core Microservices

🔐

Customer Identity Service

Manages customer profiles, KYC status, risk tiers, document storage. Source of truth for all identity data. Integrates with eKYC vendors (Jumio, Onfido, Socure).

💱

FX Rate Service

Polls liquidity providers for mid-market rates. Caches rates with TTL. Applies spread matrix per corridor and customer tier. Manages rate lock and expiry logic.

📋

Transaction Service

Orchestrates the full transaction lifecycle. Handles state transitions, idempotency, and event publishing. The system of record for all transfers.

🛡️

Compliance Engine

Rule-based engine for regulatory checks. Manages OFAC screening, AML rules, corridor restrictions, CTR/SAR triggers. Configurable without code deployments.

🤖

Fraud Detection Service

ML model serving layer. Real-time scoring with sub-100ms SLA. Feature store fed by behavioral analytics, device intelligence, and transaction history.

🏦

Partner Connectivity Service

Manages integrations with all payout partners. Abstracts protocol differences (REST, SOAP, SFTP). Handles retry logic, circuit breakers, and failover routing.

📊

Accounting / Ledger Service

Double-entry bookkeeping engine. Maintains per-corridor ledgers, Nostro position tracking, and fee revenue accounting. Feeds general ledger for financial reporting.

⚖️

Limits & Velocity Service

Atomic counter management for per-customer, per-corridor limits. Uses Redis for distributed real-time counters. Prevents race conditions in high-throughput environments.

🔔

Notification Service

Multi-channel delivery: email, SMS, push notifications. Template management per corridor/language. Regulatory receipt generation. Delivery tracking and retry.

Message Queue Architecture

Remittance platforms rely heavily on asynchronous, event-driven processing to achieve resilience and scalability. A message broker (Apache Kafka in high-scale environments, RabbitMQ or AWS SQS in smaller deployments) forms the backbone.

Idempotency & Fault Tolerance

Financial transactions require strict idempotency guarantees. Key patterns used in production systems:

  • Idempotency keys: Every API call carries a client-generated UUID. Duplicate requests with the same key return the cached response without re-processing.
  • Saga pattern: Distributed transactions across microservices use compensating transactions (refund, reversal) if any step fails downstream.
  • Optimistic locking: Database records use version columns to prevent concurrent updates causing inconsistent state.
  • Circuit breakers: Partner API integrations wrapped in circuit breakers (Hystrix/Resilience4j). If a partner fails repeatedly, traffic is cut to prevent cascading failures.
  • Exponential backoff with jitter: Retry logic for partner API calls uses randomized backoff to avoid thundering-herd problems.

Section 07

Settlement & Reconciliation Layer

Nostro / Vostro Account Model

ℹ Definition A Nostro account is an account that a financial institution holds at a foreign bank in that foreign country's currency (literally "our account at your bank"). A Vostro account is the mirror perspective — the foreign bank's view of that same account ("your account at our bank"). In remittance, operators pre-fund Nostro accounts in destination country currencies to facilitate rapid payout.

For each active corridor, a remittance operator maintains a pre-funded account at a local bank or payout partner in the destination country. This account holds local currency and is debited with each payout. The originating operator must continuously replenish these accounts to maintain liquidity.

Settlement Cycle Architecture

  1. 1
    Intraday Transaction Accounting Each payout debits the Nostro balance in real time (or near-real-time). The accounting service maintains a real-time position of funds available per corridor, tracked in an in-memory store (Redis) and persisted to the ledger database.
  2. 2
    End-of-Day Settlement File Generation At EOD cut-off, the payout partner generates a settlement file listing all paid transactions, their amounts, local currency, and references. The originating PSP generates a corresponding file from its own system. The two files are reconciled.
  3. 3
    Reconciliation & Breaks Management Automated reconciliation engine matches partner records to internal records by transaction ID. Unmatched items (breaks) classified as: timing difference (within tolerance), amount mismatch (requires investigation), partner records transaction as paid but internal record shows processing (status discrepancy). Break resolution SLA typically 24–48 hours.
  4. 4
    Replenishment / Nostro Funding When corridor balance falls below a defined minimum (e.g., 2 days' average payout volume), the treasury team initiates a replenishment SWIFT wire or API-based transfer to top up the Nostro account. Automated alerts and, in some systems, automated SWIFT MT202 messages trigger replenishment without manual intervention.
  5. 5
    FX Position Management The operator carries FX risk between the time a rate is locked for a customer and the time it settles. For high-volume operators, FX exposure is hedged using forward contracts or FX swaps through a treasury counterparty. Net open positions are marked to market daily.

Transaction State Machine

Every remittance transaction progresses through a defined set of states. State transitions are one-directional (with the exception of ON_HOLD) and all changes are recorded with timestamps and actor references in an immutable audit log.

PENDING → FUNDS_COLLECTED → PROCESSING → PAID
StateDescriptionTriggered ByNext Possible States
PENDING Transaction record created; funds not yet collected; all validations passed Customer submits transfer FUNDS_COLLECTED, CANCELLED
FUNDS_COLLECTED Sender's payment method successfully debited; funds held in operator collection account Payment processor confirmation PROCESSING, FAILED
PROCESSING Payout instruction dispatched to partner; awaiting confirmation of credit Partner API acknowledgement PAID, FAILED, ON_HOLD, RETURNED
PAID Partner confirms recipient account has been credited; terminal success state Partner webhook / status poll — (terminal state)
ON_HOLD Transaction suspended pending compliance review (OFAC hit, AML flag, or partner-initiated hold) Compliance engine, partner callback PROCESSING (if cleared), CANCELLED (if rejected)
FAILED Transaction could not be completed (partner rejection, routing failure, account closed) Partner error response REFUNDED
RETURNED Partner attempted payout but it was returned by receiving bank (wrong account, dormant account) Partner return notification REFUNDED
CANCELLED Transaction cancelled before payout — by customer (within cancellation window) or by compliance Customer request, compliance rejection REFUNDED (if FUNDS_COLLECTED)
REFUNDED Funds returned to sender; terminal state following failure, return, or cancellation Refund process completion — (terminal state)

Security & Regulatory Considerations

Information Security Requirements

🔒

Data Encryption

All PII encrypted at rest (AES-256) and in transit (TLS 1.2+). Card data tokenized per PCI-DSS. Database encryption with key management via HSM (Hardware Security Module).

🪪

Identity & Access Management

MFA required for all admin access. Role-based access control (RBAC) with least-privilege principles. Privileged Access Management (PAM) for database and infrastructure access.

📝

Audit Logging

All system events, API calls, and data access logged to immutable audit trail. Logs retained per regulatory requirements (5–7 years). SIEM integration for real-time alerting.

🌐

API Security

OAuth 2.0 / JWT authentication. Rate limiting per IP and API key. Input validation and output encoding to prevent injection. API gateway with WAF for perimeter defense.

💳

PCI-DSS Compliance

Level 1 PCI-DSS compliance for card data handling. Annual QSA audit. Card data never stored in application databases — tokenized via certified vault provider.

🔍

Penetration Testing

Quarterly automated vulnerability scanning. Annual third-party penetration testing. Bug bounty program. Responsible disclosure policy. SDLC security reviews (SAST/DAST).

Section 12

Conclusion

International remittance systems represent some of the most complex financial infrastructure in existence — operating across multiple jurisdictions, regulatory regimes, currencies, and technology stacks simultaneously. A single transaction that appears instantaneous to the end customer involves dozens of automated validation, screening, and routing decisions occurring in parallel within milliseconds.

The core lifecycle can be understood in five broad phases:

  1. Identity & Risk Establishment — KYC, sanctions screening, fraud scoring and risk-tiering at onboarding and re-evaluated at every transaction
  2. Transaction Initiation & Validation — FX quoting, compliance rule evaluation, limit checks, and atomic fund collection with idempotency guarantees
  3. Cross-Border Dispatch & Routing — Async event-driven submission to correspondent partners, with retry and circuit-breaker resilience patterns
  4. Inbound Payout & Credit — Local compliance re-screening, payout rail selection, recipient account credit via domestic payment infrastructure
  5. Settlement, Reconciliation & Liquidity Management — Nostro/Vostro position management, EOD settlement file reconciliation, and proactive corridor replenishment

The industry is undergoing significant transformation driven by three forces: instant payment rails (UPI, FPS, RTP, PIX) enabling real-time cross-border credit; regulatory modernization (ISO 20022 migration, open banking APIs, CBDC pilots) improving interoperability and traceability; and fintech disaggregation (Wise, Remitly, Nium) leveraging local collection models and API-first architecture to undercut traditional agent network operators on cost and speed.

For system architects designing or evaluating a remittance platform, the critical design principles are: immutable audit trails, idempotent operations, event-driven decoupling, real-time compliance at every state transition, and proactive liquidity monitoring. Missing any of these in production creates either regulatory exposure, operational failure, or both.

✓ Key Takeaway The most resilient remittance architectures treat compliance not as a gate at the beginning of a transaction, but as a continuous, layered process that runs at onboarding, at each transaction, during processing, and in post-transaction batch analysis. The regulatory and technical obligations are inseparable.
